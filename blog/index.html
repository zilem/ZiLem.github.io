S H A R E        

Why Film?

My love affair with story.

I FIRST DISCOVERED film as a 25 year old art student in New York. It struck me then that film, the study of human drama in creative form, could be the ultimate medium of self-expression I was searching for.

Having careered in many fields, from architecture and engineering to fashion, food, finance and consulting, none has proved as rewarding as understanding and sculpting human behavior and emotions through storytelling.

I used to be obsessed with the American indie thinking man’s Sci-Fi; films that commonly explored a what-if situation based on a scientific concept.

But as I survived each debilitating crisis: a divorce, losing a partner to cancer, I appreciate in real life, one of screenwriting’s major tenets - character transformation. I could no longer return to making pure fiction based on imaginary concepts.


I longed to touch, move and inspire people through drama that have real-life consequences.

SCREENWRITING, THE CONCEPTION of story and drama, has became for me a useful tool for understanding life and for creating authentic brand identity.

Today, I find myself increasingly drawn to true stories with compelling causes. They become the unifying theme across all the stories I tell. It keeps me questioning my values, my motivations and my insecurities. By remaining true to what I stand for, I create only stories that resonate with my audience on a deep emotional level.


S H A R E        

Why True Stories?

Inherently transformational.

IF YOU WITNESS a spacecraft crash upon launch, belly violently ripped open, mechanical guts exposed, the moment is undeniably horrific...

...and as it lifts off, floating towards the vacuous heavens, towed by a giant jellyfish-like balloon, incredibly surreal. Almost seems like a scene straight out of the movie Up. Except...

...debris from the crash reveal communications and crashables that were meant to ensure the astronaut's safe return to Earth. Now you're overcome with a dreadful thought. Is the astronaut paralyzed if still alive? Or will he be dead on arrival?

For two harrowing hours in the remote desert of Alice Springs, Australia, I along with others on the team to send the first Singaporean to space experience tragedy unfold before our very eyes.

Encountering a near death experience is nothing short of life changing.

SUCH MOMENTS DRIVE home the impact of true stories, reinforcing a gut feeling I’ve had for years - pure fiction rarely reaches this level of truth. I've been blessed as a screenwriter-filmmaker to have struck narrative gold with two such stories.

Renowned screenwriter William Goldman once said,

“A Hollywood blockbuster tells us the lie we want to believe.
An indie film tells us the truth we don’t want to.”

The beauty of true stories lies in the vagaries of life, where motives are driven by real-life complexities and human contradictions. A reflection of the morally ambiguous world we inhabit. My approach harnesses the inherent dramatic quality of true stories and delivers them in its raw, unadulterated state.

True stories are transformational not for the truth it unearths.

But for the meaning it unveils.

Discovering My Why.

Clarity in my Cause.

THERE WAS A time when my life revolved around crewing on film sets. The love for filmmaking started out with the act of making films.

I figured out what I was passionate about.

As the years passed, I realized that filmmaking was about storytelling. And it all began, ironically, outside the film set. In the writer’s seat.

I figured out how to go about fulfilling what I was passionate about.

After making a few films and not totally loving it, it occurred to me that it didn’t matter if I could write and direct well. I was given a megaphone but I had no voice.

I couldn’t figure out why I was making films. I didn't know my raison d’être.


BEFORE FILM, I had given up on many things in life. Somehow, I couldn't seem to give up on film. Film was my art. Art was my cause. Serving that cause gave my life meaning.

I was adamant that if I had to give up something, it was my relationships, including my marriage, in service of my art.

At the turning point of my life, it hit me hard when I lost someone close to cancer. It struck me then that it wasn't about giving up anything. It was simply about giving. Giving more of myself.

I found clarity in my why.

Giving was my why.
Storytelling was my how.
Filmmaking was my what.

For once, there was clarity in my cause.


EVERYONE WHO KNOWS me sees me as an incorrigible idealist. I believe that by giving ourselves to a higher cause, we find greater meaning & fulfillment in our lives.

I aim to move & inspire the everyman with stories of higher causes being born out of broken people operating under morally ambiguous circumstances. Heroes are born out of the darkest point in their journeys.

In life, there are no inherently good or bad characters.

Only our judgments about others that make them so.

Film is my medium of choice for sharing these true stories that explore compelling social causes.

S H A R E        

5 Wizards.

A Holistic Approach to Illuminating Blind Spots.

DO YOU HAVE 5 people in your life you can turn to, whose perspectives you value, within 48 hours of encountering a crisis?

Are your 5 wizards wired differently enough from you, and from one another, such that their perspectives work in concert to illuminate your blind spots holistically?

Wizards don't have to be mentors or coaches. But they shouldn't be your father figure. You want a distinctive voice you trust. Not the default voice you revere. For some, their children are their wizards.

Wizarding, unlike mentoring or coaching, is a two way street. You can be a wizard to someone who's your wizard. It's not about getting advice from a sage. Not about obtaining consensus. It's about variety. Because variety induces holism.

Who you choose to be your wizards is absolutely critical. So limit those voices to 5. Most would have trouble identifying 3. View each crisis as an opportunity to reinvent yourself when you consult your wizards, and when you are someone else's wizard. The desired outcome is a lighter, enlightened mind.


S H A R E        

Fatal Flaw.

When your perceived strength limits you.

YOUR FATAL FLAW is a shortcoming that is significant because it masquerades as your strength. A quality you use to justify your success. As a result, it becomes your blind spot.

For artists, it's perfectionism. The perfection that artists seek inevitably causes them to procrastinate presenting their art to the public.

For entrepreneurs, it's optimism. An overly favorable outlook of how their product impacts their audience makes them blind to possible pitfalls.

For others, it’s diligence. The self-gratification derived from working hard makes them blind to the virtues of working smart.

For me, it's all of the above and self-righteousness. My bias towards my endeavours having deeper meaning than those of others. Sometimes, I forget that meaning is subjective.

Confirm your fatal flaw with your mentors, coaches and wizards. Have them call you out on your fatal flaw.


S H A R E        

The Unnatural Behaviour underlying Attraction.

Committing before getting to know one another.

CONSIDER THE SCENARIO in which A and B hit it off within two dates. The heightened connection experienced within a short timeframe causes them to attribute the magic to “chemistry.” They assume chemistry is a reliable indicator of long-term compatibility and they rush into a committed relationship.

Shortly after, A deluges B with texts. A believes baring the soul is an act of authenticity. A even guilt-trips B for acting guarded and aloof. Confused, B sees A as needy. Friends advise A to text less and "play the game." At best, the relationship crashes and burns. At worst, it drags begrudgingly into marriage with kids. What became of chemistry?

What should've been the right remedy? Baring the soul? Or playing the game?

NEITHER. BOTH SOLUTIONS target mismatched expectations which is the symptom. Not the cause. It is fixing the how without knowing the why.

The cause is mistaking initial chemistry for long term compatibility.

Some spend the rest of their relationship defending their initial decision based on chemistry.

The cause is also choosing commitment first, getting to know each other later.

Isn't it unnatural that we deceive ourselves that the other person is the right one for us before we discover for ourselves who they are? Yet,

Dating has been widely practiced as this unnatural form of human behaviour.

Both causes end up with us justifying our own actions to no one other than ourselves.

When we recognize that social norms have pulled a fast one on us, what is the remedy?

IT BEGINS WITH acknowledging that chemistry may act as a great initial catalyst but is ultimately a lousy indicator of long term compatibility.

Taking the time to get to know one another. Time in this context does not refer to duration spent. It refers to genuine selfless intent.

Take for instance the scenario at the beginning. Isn't A’s expectation of B’s behaviour an imposition of A’s own brand of authenticity?

Expecting the other person to get our point of view when we want them to is as ridiculous as going to the bus stop in the middle of the night expecting the bus to arrive at the very same moment. In short, timing.

Operating from our own point of reference is always convenient, self-righteous and self-centered. Instead, why don't we try asking...

Am I interested in knowing the other person or am I more interested in being myself?

Start with a tad of unconditional love. When you're open to how the other person thinks, behaves, and makes decisions, you respect them for what they stand for. This mitigates mismatched expectations of the other person's behaviour.

When you get to know one another with genuine selfless intent, you’re still being yourself. Just not indulging in it. You acknowledge the difference between the two of you without judgment.

You're comfortable with that difference. Nothing more needs to be done.


Note: If you've bothered reading so far, all of the above applies to deep collaborative relationships with anyone.


S H A R E        

Emotional Needs.

Knowing what drives you in life.

MEDITATIVE SCREENWRITING TEACHER Jessica Hinds introduced the concept of the 5 prime emotional needs underlying every action that a character takes.

Validation - to prove to yourself
Respect - to prove to others
Justice - to fight against injustice
Love - to be with someone you love
Comfort - to be in the company of friends

Emotional needs are not tied to the nature of the action. It's not about what you're doing but why you're doing what you're doing.

If you're climbing Mount Everest, most would assume it’s for validation. That maybe true for most cases. Though you could be climbing Mount Everest for any of the following reasons:

To challenge yourself - Validation
To gain the respect of others - Respect
To champion a cause - Justice
To be with your spouse - Love
To be amongst friends - Comfort

Some actions have up to three underlying emotional needs but one stands out as dominant.

TAKING THIS CONCEPT one step further and applying it to your life’s journey, you can ask yourself...

Has my life been about the pursuit of Validation, Respect, Justice, Love or Comfort?

As you begin to rank the top three emotional needs that drive your core, you get a better sense of why you do the things you do.

My primary emotional need in life began with Respect till my twenties. Validation in my thirties. At the turning point in my life, it became about Justice.

What drives you in life?


S H A R E        

Character Transformation.

When you don’t get what you want, but what you need.

PERHAPS THE MOST significant element in all great films that touch, move and inspire audiences - Character transformation goes beyond merely seeing a person acquire a new skill or adopt a different persona at the end of the film.

To see this powerful concept executed in screenwriting, a highly driven character (e.g. an entrepreneur) begins his journey with an objective - a notion of what he wants. He is undeterred from his goal. The audience roots for him because of his determination.

By the end of his journey, he arrives at an unexpected yet inevitable conclusion that what he wants no longer matters. Instead he discovers what he needs. Usually something that fills an obvious emotional void. This bitter-sweet moment can be so unforgettable for the audience that it has the capacity to change lives.


Individuals in real life are subjected to the same emotional gravity as characters in films.

HERE'S A TRUE story. I was working on the screenplays of two true stories with extremely compelling causes when I found out that my partner was stricken with terminal cancer. It was a rare form of cancer that showed few external signs of debilitation. I took it for granted that she had a few more years.

Given the extreme difficulties of getting a film produced, I plowed on my screenplay tirelessly, prioritizing art over love. Despite her pleas, I took off for a month to co-write with my writing partner in Los Angeles.

When I returned, I was shocked to find her body muscles have atrophied beyond belief. Her condition declined rapidly thereafter. In a few months, I was overcame with shock when she could no longer recognize me. Shortly after, she passed on.

Could I not have waited a year to make my trip to LA?

Unfortunately that was all hindsight. What I wanted badly was to complete my screenplay. Call it Art. Ambition. Achievement. What I needed most was compassion. Regrettably, I learnt it the hard way and bore the brunt of a painful, irreversible transformation.


IN THE MOST popular TV series of our time, Game of Thrones, we find ourselves gripped by the unpredictable events that happen to the characters. What makes the series such a success is the unpredictable transformation that results in the characters.

All great character transformations are unexpected yet inevitable.

The Problem with Self-Esteem.

When you don't know what you stand for.

BASED ON MASLOW'S Hierarchy of Needs, how do you deal with problems of Self-Esteem?

Many professionals like to believe they're not making enough money. Occasionally, I feel the same way too. Say I establish that my money issues aren't related to Safety (i.e. financial security), but to Esteem (i.e. self-esteem).

Acquiring more money will only perpetuate Esteem issues. It's an addiction that arises from a place of fear. A fear of inadequacy.

Consider reframing the problem of Self-Esteem as one of Self-Actualization.

When I determine what I stand for, I begin to operate at the level of Self-Actualization. I stand for Meaning so I ask...

Is my current pursuit of more money meaningful? If not, does it make sense to be addicted to making money I don't need? Only to perpetuate that fear of inadequacy?

Our objective in life is to ascend the pyramid. Not descend.

WHEN YOU STRUGGLE with issues of Self-Esteem, you’re shackled by the status quo. The social norms that you subconsciously partake in perpetuating. You're measuring yourself against the standards of others.

When you struggle with issues of Self-Actualization, you’re actively questioning the status quo. Disrupting those very shackling social norms. You're measuring yourself against the standards of your own.

If you're a parent, do you want your children to be struggling with issues of Self-Esteem? Or do you want them actively pursing what they Stand For?

Don't forget your children are constantly looking up to you as their role model, wondering...

Are you Pursuing what you Stand For?

The Pro Bono Professional.

Autonomy. Competency. Community.

BEHAVIOURAL ECONOMIST Dan Ariely points out that we live simultaneously in two worlds. Social norms & Market norms. Community favours vs Financial transactions.

The world you define a relationship in determines its behaviour.

Pro bono professionals are not employees or volunteers who forgo compensation for drudgery. They choose to spend time...

Exploring creative meaningful solutions for a greater purpose.

On their own terms.

Introduce money and you take that away from them. What was once play now becomes work, upending performance & creativity. Market norms enter. Social norms depart. Too little money, they feel insulted. Too much, they feel used. Both sides become calculative about their resources. The relationship is unnecessarily strained.

Meaning is the strongest way to motivate people.

Money the most expensive.

PRO BONO PROFESSIONALS get to constantly question what the project means for them and why they are in it. The autonomy to achieve competency within a community of like-minded individuals is what drives the pro bono professional.

When I work pro bono, because I'm not paid, to justify my time, I seek to be as involved as I am with commissioned work. I expect the same from the client. If I sense that the client is not respecting my time or my perspective, I'm prepared to walk away.

Pro bono is not an excuse to exploit free labour.

Movement beyond Brand, Change beyond Achievement.

Redefining your audience’s identity.

A GREAT BRAND positions itself well. Differentiates itself from its competitors. Derives its brand identity based on its philosophy. Establishing a known brand is a remarkable achievement.

A social movement champions a cause for the greater good. Doesn't concern itself with competitors. Defines its audience's identity based on its philosophy. Establishing a compelling movement produces lasting change in society.


BEING A DISRUPTOR is fashionable these days. But there's no authentic power in allowing your identity to be defined only by what the industry is not.

Apple & Virgin are commonly referred to as lifestyle brands. They’re stylish. But styles go out of style. And eventually out of business. Apple & Virgin don’t.

Apple & Virgin are grounded in their cause of challenging the status quo. Differentiation from their competitors is a result. Not a motivation. Beauty and innovation is an outcome. Not an impetus.

If we consider Apple & Virgin as disruptors, we need to ask how many markets have they upended? Like a vigilante, a disruptor can be ignored.

Apple & Virgin however, are movements beyond a brand. Their identities are moulded by a higher cause. Their achievements are merely by-products of our paradigm shift.

When you create a compelling movement, a force that can't be ignored, your audience can’t resist deriving their identity from your cause. This alignment results in change beyond achievement.

Past the tipping point, your movement becomes legend.


S H A R E        

Taking Action over Obsessing the Outcome.

We learn who we truly are should we fail.

ON 31 MAY 2019, maverick scientist Lim Seng led a team of volunteers on a noteworthy attempt to send the first Singaporean to space in a garage spacecraft.

He failed to make it to space. But he succeeded in making it back alive.

Why is this significant?

I BEGAN COVERING Lim Seng's story as an independent filmmaker in early 2018. Moved by his intention to inspire the next generation of Singaporeans to take action on their dreams, I came onboard pro bono as brand strategist to promote his cause. His first attempt to launch a manned flight to space was on 15 May 2018 from Alice Springs, Australia.

In the days leading to the launch, Singapore’s first astronaut was revealed. Volunteers along with their family members and a news crew from Singapore showed up in the remote desert to show their support. Unfortunately, winter came early that year. Winds were too strong for a safe liftoff. Everyone went home with a heavy heart when we failed to launch.

A year later, the team of volunteers and news crew reconvened in Alice Springs for the second attempt on 26 April 2019. Only this time, the team was smaller. Weather conditions were favourable leading up to the launch date. But owing to malfunctioning navigation equipment, once again, we failed to launch.

CONSIDERING THE CONSEQUENCE of failure for most startups rarely places a human’s life in harm’s way, Lim Seng has been rightfully prudent for safety reasons. However, patience was running thin amongst his volunteers. With two failed attempts to launch and social media deriding it as a scam, the raison d'être of the project and his credibility as a man of action were now being tested.

His moral dilemma became about striking a balance between safety and credibility.

As it turns out, safety in a never been done before project like this one, can be a constant moving target. Within a fortnight, Lim Seng rallied his troops with the promise that he would launch on 31 May 2019. Earnestly reaching out to every volunteer, he assembled a skeletal crew of nine for his third attempt. I was one of them, assigned to play an integral role in operations.

A day prior to the launch, Lim Seng announced the inevitable. Given the risks involved, the most responsible course of action as campaign director was for him, the man who funded the venture and who engineered the spacecraft, to assume the role of astronaut provided he deemed it safe enough for launch. To him, it was. Finally,

Man and machine became one, resolving his moral dilemma.

AS THE LAUNCH kicked into high gear, unlike previous unmanned launches, every action from inflating the stratospheric ballon to stabilizing the spacecraft had tremendous consequence if gone wrong. Simply because the stakes were so bloody high. The payload wasn't a piece of test equipment. It was a living breathing human being! Who must return to Earth. Who must return alive.

Murphy’s law “anything can go wrong will go wrong” interfered relentlessly. Upon liftoff, an unforeseen ground impact led to the loss of communications equipment. Worse, it compromised the integrity of the spacecraft. Depressurization kicked in. Mission was aborted thirty minutes later upon ascending to half the intended altitude.

No one on the ground had an inkling if Lim Seng was alive for two harrowing hours since liftoff till search & rescue found him intact among the spacecraft debris. Many lessons were learned that day. We failed to achieve our objective. But, we did not fail to launch.

We did not fail to take action.

MOST PEOPLE FAIL to dream because they're either burdened by the fact that they have to dream big or they can't tolerate failure. In other words,

They are preoccupied with the image of success.

If success came easy, all we get is an overinflated sense of self, vindicated for having proven to the world what we’ve set out to accomplish. On the other hand, if the outcome didn't matter as much, we're less paralyzed to take action. And should we fail, we're able to shift our focus from worrying about our egos to learning about who we truly are.

Where the phrase “the process is more important than the goal” is about the appreciation of the journey, “taking action over obsessing the outcome” is about having the courage to embark on that journey.

My Process.

Moving & Inspiring audiences through their hero’s journey.

WHAT MAKES A good promo video? Whether the objective is fund-raising or growing awareness, my process employs feature film storytelling techniques that tug at audiences' heartstrings.

Assuming you, the client, has already established both a clearly defined objective as well as the cause (e.g. World Vision wants donations of $45/month towards child sponsorship in developing countries).



1. Who is your intended audience?

No promo is successful unless it moves & inspires your audience. Not just any audience. An intended audience is an audience you define specifically with intent, and whose participation as a result of viewing the promo can hopefully, be measured. Simply casting a wide net is at best, a shot in the dark.



2. Which heroes do your intended audience look up to?

Half the battle is won simply by featuring a hero (screenwriter's term that refers to the main character of the promo) who is already well-regarded by your intended audience. All the better if their hero is known to be aligned with your cause.



3. What is the hero’s own personal journey?

As a writer, I interview the hero for his/her personal journey specific to the cause. I’m seeking goals, villains, stakes and triumphs that the intended audience can identify with as part of their common struggle.

Somewhere in that mutual exploration, the truth recedes and meaning emerges. This informs the Director's Statement, which is a perspective unique to the filmmaker. Here's an example. Crafting a script using this approach beats any script I can possibly come up with based on pure imagination.



4. Getting the hero into character.

The hero is comprised of the person in real life and the character in the promo. In reality, the person never spends 24/7 living the life of the character.

As a director, regardless if the hero is an actor, I ensure that the hero delivers a performance on camera that is perceived as authentic to the character and not merely regurgitating lines.

This "Authentic Performance", oxymoronic as it may sound, is essential to capturing the audience’s imagination and igniting their passion. It is also the final step that culminates the creative collaboration between the hero/actor and the writer-director.



Putting this Process into Perspective.

What we're doing is raising funds or awareness for the cause.

How we're doing it is via the hero's journey the audience can identify with.

Why we're doing it is to give the audience a higher cause to stand for.

We should never lose track of why we do what we do.



S H A R E        

Story vs Storytelling.

How you tell it matters.

STORY IS FOUND in the history that exists in every character, person or company. Identifying a good story is important. But it won’t matter unless you know how to tell it well. Here’s an example.

Spoilers ahead for the character of Jaime Lannister the Kingslayer in the popular TV series Game of Thrones.

IN SEASON 1, Jaime Lannister is being introduced as the villain knight in shining armor who is caught in an incestuous act with his sister by a ten year old. Jaime pushes the boy from the window, crippling him for the rest of his life.

A few episodes later, Jaime’s overbearing father berates Jaime for the shame he's harboured years after slaying the king he was sworn to protect. Jaime experiences brief moments of disillusionment about the notion of honour he’s meant to uphold as a knight.

Throughout season 2 whilst being captured as a prisoner of war, Jaime sacrifices his cousin’s life in his attempts to escape. In the face of death, he continues to display malice, depravity and brutality - traits of the ultimate irredeemable character.

After spending two seasons with Jamie Lannister, this is clearly someone who despite many life-changing experiences, including an interesting kingslaying story, remains largely a cardboard villain.

What's the point of Jaime's story?

COME SEASON 3, the unimaginable happens. While being escorted by Brienne to the capital, they both get captured. Jaime defends Brienne from rape and consequently gets his sword hand chopped off. A fate far worse than death for a knight.

With a severed hand dangling over his neck, he loses all sense of purpose, dignity and identity. He descends to an unforeseen state of vulnerability. Like a patient suffering from PTSD, he opens up to Brienne about a secret - he slayed the king to save his father and thousands of innocent lives.

Jaime's story has always been there.

But it only begins to matter from this point. It begins to matter for Jaime. And Jaime begins to matter for the audience. From this point on, he becomes a different man. He develops greater empathy for others, strives to act more honorably, and would go on to become a character that audiences root for.

Jaime Lannister’s transformation makes him a rare, memorable character in cinematic history. Not simply because of what happened to him. But because of how his story is told - his lifelong struggle between being loyal to his family and being the honourable knight.


IT'S NOT ENOUGH to discover a good story. As a filmmaker of true stories, I ask others as I ask myself, Which moments do you choose to dramatize? Which moments do you choose to fictionalize?

A good story is a diamond in the rough.

Only good storytelling can unearth it.

S H A R E        

Are people interested in the Truth?

Stories are about Meaning. Not about Truth.

IF FACTS REPRESENT reality, Truth is a perspective of that reality, and Meaning is a selective interpretation of that perspective of that reality.

Even in writing true stories, I occasionally fall into the trap of believing I'm telling the truth. A cold hard look reveals that I'm selectively interpreting the truth, which results in meaning.

Truth and Meaning as it turns out, are not the same thing. I can’t be telling both. The audience may think they came for the Truth. But truth be told, they want Meaning.

Because I began with the assumption that Facts represent reality, in life, the Truth is rarely as relevant as the Meaning we choose to take away from it.

Church, Craft & Craving.

A Powerful Paradigm for Artists & Startups.

THE THREE VITAL elements for convincing your audience. CRAFT is the technical knowhow required to accomplish the objective. It is the How?

CRAVING is the hunger beyond passion that drives the journey. It is powered by your unique perspective based on what you stand for. It is the Why?

CHURCH refers to an individual or organization highly regarded by your audience that gives you split-second credibility & legitimacy when you can show their endorsement of you.


S H A R E        

Facts are meaningless.

Stories are meaningful.

HUMAN BEINGS HAVE the innate blessing, and flaw of associating subjective meaning with mere facts. Almost always involuntarily.

We convince ourselves of such meaning by believing the stories we tell ourselves. Thankfully, this is why moving and inspiring films have the ability to transform an audience.

As brand strategist on the space endeavour, the Facts are a man in a box rises to the sky before falling back to Earth. The Story we tell ourselves is a Singaporean goes to Space.

We built a following because 'Singapore' obviously meant something to our audience. But that story didn't land for other Singaporeans. Perhaps they weren't our target audience. Perhaps it didn't mean as much to them. So we ask ourselves...

How can we tell this story more meaningfully?

S H A R E        

The Culture of Failure.

Sexy for startups. Stigma for marriages.

ONCE UPON A time, it was a no-no to admit failure in business. Silicon Valley changed all that. The fail-fast culture took hold. Now failure has its own conference, FailCon and global movement, Fuckup Nights.

Singapore purportedly supports innovation yet demands “latest proven technology.” What is latest hasn't been proven. What is proven can't be the latest. It's no wonder the most commonly asked questions are, “Who has done this before?” and worse, “If it’s such a great idea, why hasn’t somebody else done it already?”

The spirit of the endeavour to send the first Singaporean to space is about embracing failure. During a team huddle, a senior advisor remarked,


“Failure is not the end of the world. It's the beginning of success.”

When the conversation drifted to more light-hearted topics, I was asked,

“Are you single?”

“Yes.”

“Why aren’t you married?”

“I used to be. Now I’m divorced.”

The room went silent. It wasn’t the response everyone was expecting. But why? If failure denotes the beginning of success, why are we ready to embrace the pariah in a failed business but not in a failed marriage?

Realistically speaking, doesn't the endless hours that founders devote to their startups take a toll on their families' well-being and finances? If startup founders are more prone to divorce, why are we more forgiving towards their business failures than their marital failures?

If we're to inspire a healthy mindset towards failure for the next generation, we shouldn't stigmatize failed marriages. As with all experiences and mistakes in life,


A failed marriage is no different from a failed startup.

It lays the foundation for the next successful one.

Intersectionality.


IN AMERICA, THE gender discrimination faced by a White Female and the racial discrimination faced by an Asian Male both stem from a toxic white masculine culture.

In Asia & Africa, a self-imposed discrimination aka “Colonial Disease” manifests itself in the local government favouring the White Man.

In Singapore, Malay & Indian minorities struggle to conform under a certain "Chinese privilege" conferred to the Chinese majority, a variant of Sinocentrism experienced in China.

Intersectionality is the Interconnected Nature of Race, Class & Gender that creates overlapping & interdependent systems of Discrimination or Disadvantage.

Pragmatism as Oppression.

"The most potent weapon of the oppressor is the mind of the oppressed."
 - Steven Biko, Anti-Apartheid Activist

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN those with the power to say no, lack the balls to say yes to true innovation?

For all that Singapore prides itself on innovation, is it destined to become a 'smart nation' of landlords, materialists & conformists? Pragmatism is the modern day shackles we must break free of. We need role models who champion ideals & autonomy.
