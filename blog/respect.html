An Epiphany about Respect.

How I lost a big brother.

I have no siblings. When I was young, I grew up with a cousin who was 3 years older than me. He was practically my big brother. He behaved like one and demanded to be respected like one.

When I went overseas for my university education and returned one summer, he noticed that I spoke with an open mind and he didn’t like it. He said, “I don’t like that just because you have the opportunity to study overseas, that now you’re better than me.” If I confronted him about this today, he probably wouldn’t recall any of it.

When I started working, I realised to my horror that I absolutely hated the corporate life. I quit after 2 years and went freelance. Meanwhile, my cousin took to corporate life like a duck to water. In his early 30s, he rose rapidly through the corporate ranks. At one point, he had several hundred workers under him. He absolutely loved that authority.

Then all of that plateaued for him. And he started to dream of side projects to run. Since I was a freelancer, I encouraged him to be entrepreneurial. But corporate life was too comfortable for him to take any action. So those dreams remained largely as dreams.

Once in a while, a couple of these projects involved setting up businesses that processed the by-products of the company that he worked for except he wouldn't declare his interest in them. He saw an opportunity that was clearly a conflict of interest. Not only was it illegal, it was highly immoral. Thankfully he didn’t take action on these either.

When he finally took action, it was on a revolutionary product that didn’t involve the company he worked for. So it’s legit. I supported him wholeheartedly. I got him to consider launching it on Kickstarter and offered to make the marketing video for him. For free.

I shared many of my creative and marketing-oriented ideas with him. But because of his manufacturing background and technical training, he didn’t appreciate any of these ideas. He didn’t see the need to develop a marketing strategy. All he wanted was for me to make a technical, boring, cookie-cutter video.

I procrastinated. For weeks. Finally, I confronted myself about my own inaction. I started by asking what I stood to gain from being a part of this project.

1. Gain experience working the Kickstarter platform.
2. Gain experience working on a commercial end-user product.
3. Prove my worth to a big brother.

That last one hit me like a lightning bolt. I was seeking respect from him. I have been seeking respect from him for the last 30 something years. And guess what? I’m never gonna get it. Because he’s never gonna give it to me. Not for this project. Not ever.

Then I unearthed something even deeper. Perhaps it was me. All these years, I wanted him to take action on his dreams so that I can finally respect him. Realising this sent chills down my spine.

Clearly, this relationship wasn’t going to work. Neither party respected the other. But why? Why was I oblivious to this for the last 30 years?

I questioned myself rigorously. And I came up with a solution. A framework for evaluating deep collaborative relationships. We know it comes down to respect. But what should we respect someone for?

We all know genuine respect is not earned through status, power or wealth. Turns out we don’t respect someone simply for knowledge, seniority or even character. We respect someone for what he/she stands for. To us.

My cousin probably thinks he stands for authority. Three hundred men under him. He needed that respect. But to me, he stands for shortcuts. Whereas I stand for meaning. The pursuit of deeper meaning in life. And no way you gonna get there via shortcuts.

I realised I had to cut him off from my life. Imagine that pain. He was my big brother! I went ahead anyway. I mentally severed my ties with him. My only regret was it took me over 30 years to realise this.
